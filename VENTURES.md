  6. Brief Overview (2000 chars)

  Problem: In South Sudan, 67% of health facilities do not report to DHIS2 — the national health information system. The country has no national ID system, less than 33% data reporting rates, widespread illiteracy among health workers, and near-zero internet connectivity at the point of care. The result: the government and international health organizations are making life-or-death resource allocation decisions with almost no data. Outbreaks go undetected. Children miss vaccines with no one tracking them. Mothers die in childbirth without the death ever being registered. Lancet research shows these data gaps are lethal: in a study of 412,173 heart failure patients, 63.8% with prior symptoms received zero recommended investigations in primary care — resulting in a 5.29x mortality hazard for those diagnosed only during emergency admission [Lawson et al., Lancet Prim Care 2025; 1: 100060].

  Solution: Taban is an offline-first electronic medical record (EMR) system purpose-built for South Sudan's five-level health system — from village Boma Health Workers managing 40 households to the Ministry of Health. It runs entirely on a $50 Android phone with no internet required. Taban includes AI-assisted clinical diagnosis (15+ disease algorithms), real-time immunization defaulter tracking, WHO-compliant birth and death registration (CRVS), antenatal care (WHO 8-contact model), and automated DHIS2 export — all designed for low-literacy users with icon-driven, binary-choice interfaces and voice capture.

  Market: We are targeting South Sudan first (2,400+ Boma health units, 800+ facilities), then scaling to fragile and low-resource health systems across East Africa and Sub-Saharan Africa that lack interoperable EMRs — a market of 47 countries and over 100,000 health facilities. A Lancet review of 380 cost-effectiveness scenarios found CHW programmes cost-effective in 78–93% of RMNCH scenarios, with returns up to 10x investment [Stansert Katzen et al., Lancet Prim Care 2025; 1: 100076]. Taban equips these CHWs with the digital tools that make integrated approaches the most cost-effective model.

  Business Model: Unlike most global health tools that depend entirely on donor funding, Taban operates a dual-engine model. Public sector deployments (government ministries + NGOs) create national coverage and health impact. Private sector licensing (clinics, hospitals, corporate health programs, pharmacies) generates sustainable commercial revenue. The architecture ensures both engines feed the same DHIS2 pipeline — so every private clinic that pays for Taban also expands national health surveillance. Making money and solving the problem are the same action.

  Traction: We have a functional MVP with 31 modules, 9 role-based dashboards, 17 offline databases, 189 automated tests, and expert validation from a South Sudan health system professional. We are preparing for a pilot deployment in one Payam (sub-county) in Central Equatoria state.

  12. Approach to Solving the Problem (2000 chars)

  Solution Architecture: Taban is a full-stack offline-first EMR built with Next.js, React, PouchDB (IndexedDB), and Expo (React Native for mobile). Every feature works without internet —
  data is stored locally and syncs when connectivity is available.

  How It Works for Each User:

  Boma Health Worker opens the app on a basic Android phone. They select a household using a geocode ID (BOMA-KJ-HH1001 — no national ID needed). They tap through an icon-driven flow:
  (1) Where → geocode, (2) Who → name + photo, (3) What → dropdown condition, (4) Action → treated or referred, (5) Outcome → recovered/died/follow-up. Voice capture records the encounter automatically. The AI Symptom Checker suggests diagnoses with confidence scores and severity — telling the worker whether to treat locally or refer urgently.

  Clinical Officers at the Payam level use the full consultation workflow: record vitals, chief complaint, physical exam findings. The AI Clinical Evaluation engine analyzes all inputs and returns ranked differential diagnoses with ICD-11 codes, suggested confirmatory lab tests, and treatment recommendations per WHO/IMCI guidelines. A Clinical Scribe converts voice recordings into structured SOAP notes automatically.

  Payam Supervisors see a real-time dashboard showing which Boma Health Workers are active, their visit counts, referral rates, and follow-up completion. They can remotely review any BHW's patient encounters and add clinical notes — creating a mentorship and quality assurance loop that has never existed in South Sudan.

  Government Administrators access a national dashboard with an interactive map of all 10 states, DHIS2 export (JSON + CSV) for 50+ health indicators, epidemic intelligence with Rt estimation and outbreak detection, vital statistics, facility assessments (WHO SARA-aligned), and MCH analytics.

  The entire system supports 9 role-based dashboards with granular permissions, ensuring each user sees exactly what they need — no more, no less.

  14. Competitive Landscape (2000 chars)

  Existing Alternatives:

  1. DHIS2 (University of Oslo): The dominant health information system used by 80+ countries. However, DHIS2 is an aggregate reporting platform, not a point-of-care EMR. It requires
  internet, is complex to use, and 67% of South Sudanese facilities cannot report to it. Taban complements DHIS2 by capturing data at the point of care and exporting it in DHIS2-compatible
   format.

  2. OpenMRS / OpenClinic: Open-source EMRs used in some African hospitals. These require server infrastructure, stable internet, and trained IT staff to deploy and maintain. They are
  designed for hospital settings, not community health workers or offline village environments. They do not support the five-level hierarchical health system structure that South Sudan
  requires.

  3. CommCare (Dimagi): A mobile data collection platform used by NGOs for community health programs. It is form-based (not a clinical EMR), requires online configuration, does not include AI-assisted diagnosis, and lacks integrated DHIS2 export, pharmacy, lab, or referral workflows. It addresses data collection but not clinical decision support.

  4. Medic Mobile (Community Health Toolkit): Focused on SMS-based community health workflows. Effective for simple task tracking but lacks clinical depth — no ICD-11 coding, no AI diagnosis, no integrated lab/pharmacy, no CRVS, no epidemic intelligence.

  5. Paper-Based Systems: The current reality in most South Sudanese facilities. Data stays in paper registers, is never digitized, never aggregated, and never reported.

  Why These Alternatives Fall Short: None of these solutions simultaneously provide: (a) offline-first operation on a $50 phone, (b) AI clinical decision support, (c) five-level health system support from village to national, (d) integrated DHIS2 export, (e) real-time immunization defaulter tracking, (f) CRVS birth/death registration, (g) geocode-based patient ID for populations without national IDs, and (h) voice capture for low-literacy users. Taban is purpose-built for exactly this gap. The value of comprehensive EHR infrastructure is demonstrated by England's CPRD database, which covers 20% of the population and enabled a study of 412,173 heart failure patients and 2,564,729 corticosteroid patients to identify systemic diagnostic failures — the same kind of population-level insight that South Sudan desperately needs [Lawson et al., 2025; Whittaker et al., Lancet Prim Care 2025; 1: 100080].

  23. Key Risk Factors (2000 chars)

  1. Political Instability and Conflict: South Sudan has experienced civil war and ongoing inter-communal violence. Active conflict in deployment areas could prevent pilot execution,
  damage or confiscate devices, or displace health workers and patients. Mitigation: Start in Central Equatoria (one of the most stable regions), design for data portability (geocode IDs
  work in IDP camps), and build the system to function independently at each level so that disruption at one level doesn't cascade.

  2. Infrastructure and Connectivity: While Taban is offline-first, device procurement, initial setup, and CouchDB sync require some infrastructure. Power is unreliable; solar charging is
  needed for many areas. Mitigation: PouchDB runs on $50 Android phones with days-long battery life. SMS fallback for critical data (immunization alerts). Solar charger can be included in
  deployment kits.

  3. User Adoption and Literacy: Health workers may resist new technology or lack basic device literacy. Civil salaries have been unpaid for over a year, reducing motivation. Mitigation:
  Icon-driven interfaces, voice capture, binary choices, and photo-based workflows minimize literacy requirements. Payam supervisor dashboard creates accountability that drives adoption.

  4. Sustainability and Funding Dependency: Initial revenue depends heavily on donor/NGO funding and government contracts, which are subject to political and budgetary cycles. Mitigation: Our dual-engine model directly addresses this. Private sector revenue (clinics, corporate health, pharmacies, telemedicine) generates commercial income independent of donor cycles. By Year 3, we target 40-60% of revenue from private sector sources. The private sector also creates a self-reinforcing adoption loop: as more private facilities join the Taban network, the data becomes more comprehensive, making the platform more valuable to government buyers — and vice versa.

  5. Data Privacy and Security: Health data is sensitive, and South Sudan lacks comprehensive data protection legislation. Mitigation: Data stored locally on devices (no cloud dependency),
   bcrypt password hashing, JWT authentication, role-based access controls, audit logging, and security headers are already implemented.

  6. Competition from Established Players: Large organizations like Dimagi (CommCare) have established relationships with donors. Mitigation: Our offline AI-assisted clinical decision
  support, five-level architecture, and deep contextual design for South Sudan create differentiation that form-based tools cannot replicate.
